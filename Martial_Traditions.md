Against the Grade --- Martial Traditions, Weapons, and Tactics Reference
========================================================================

Technology Level
----------------

Alinar's civilization developed from a zero-knowledge starting point ---
children under ten with no technical education, supplemented only by
DNA-embedded survival instincts calibrated to the planet. Over \~1,300 years of
independent development, the population rediscovered fire management, basic
metallurgy, agriculture, and animal domestication before any weapons technology
could emerge. The DNA-embedded survival instincts dramatically compressed the
earliest stages of this process compared to Earth's timeline.

At \~1,300 years with those advantages, the civilization has reached
high-quality steel production, early plate armor for wealthy elites, and
mechanical crossbows. There is no gunpowder. No inherited chemistry tradition or
alchemical research lineage exists to produce it. The civilization is
metalworking-advanced but chemistry-primitive --- roughly analogous to late
medieval / early renaissance metallurgy, with the caveat that the development
path was non-linear. Some technologies were skipped, others stumbled into based
on local materials and the specific pressures of the Ulreth threat.

The Two Fundamental Combat Problems
-----------------------------------

Every technique on Alinar is a response to one of two very different combat
problems. The techniques that work against one problem are often wrong for the
other. This tension is where the class and cultural divide in martial tradition
originates.

**Problem One: Ulreth.** Physically robust opponents, often in groups, ranging
from animal-dumb to disturbingly clever, driven by territorial instinct rather
than strategic objectives. They don't surrender, retreat unpredictably, and the
more advanced ones use rudimentary tools. They are not trying to defeat humans
--- they are defending territory or scavenging. Their aggression has triggers
and thresholds.

**Problem Two: Humans.** The Provings, feudal enforcement, and later organized
warfare. Opponents who think, plan, use sophisticated weapons, and can be
intimidated, negotiated with, or broken psychologically. Human combat is about
imposing your will on someone who has a will of their own.

Weapons by Class and Context
----------------------------

### Frontier / Herder Kit

A frontier herder's equipment reflects poverty and practicality. Every item
serves multiple purposes because poor communities cannot afford single-purpose
weapons.

-   **Spear.** The dominant frontier weapon. Cheap to produce (self-made shaft,
    traded iron point). Keeps physically robust attackers at distance. Can be
    used from behind a fence or palisade. Works against individuals and groups.
    Doubles as a walking staff in mountain terrain. Herders like Baret would
    carry one as standard equipment.
-   **Heavy knife or hand axe.** The most valuable weapon a herder owns,
    probably inherited. Broad-bladed, designed for catastrophic damage at close
    range rather than finesse. Also used for clearing brush and processing wood.
-   **Sling.** Essentially free --- a herder's tool that doubles as a weapon.
    Ammunition is literally lying on the ground. Mountain herders would know
    slings intimately. Effective for Ulreth deterrence at range.

### Settlement Defense Kit

Frontier settlements invest in communal defense infrastructure rather than
individual arms.

-   **Crossbows.** Valuable for wall defense. Require less training than
    longbows. More expensive and slower, making them a rich-fortress investment
    rather than a poor-village one.
-   **Longbows.** Essential for Ulreth deterrence at range. Require more
    individual training investment than crossbows but are cheaper to produce.
-   **Trap lines.** Pitfalls, sharpened stakes, deadfalls, fire lines,
    noise-makers. A settlement's first line of defense is the engineered
    landscape around it. Pakurdan (fortress + valley) implies defensive
    geography is already embedded in naming conventions.

### Feudal / Retainer Kit

Haldrin's retainers carry equipment that reflects economic privilege and a
martial tradition oriented around human-vs-human combat.

-   **Swords or long knives.** Status weapons. Expensive, better steel, designed
    for the individual blade combat the Provings test. These are purpose-built
    weapons, not dual-purpose tools.
-   **Quality armor.** Leather at minimum, metal components for those who can
    afford it. Early plate elements for the wealthiest retainers.
-   **Purpose-built weapons.** The retainer class can afford equipment that
    serves only one function --- fighting humans.

### Thematic Significance of the Class Divide

The weapon gap between classes is visible and politically resonant. Haldrin's
system sorts people through combat trials using weapons most candidates can
barely afford. The frontier's real martial knowledge --- surviving Ulreth
encounters, reading terrain, setting defensive perimeters --- is treated as
low-class practicality, not warrior skill. Baret's herding instincts saving
Barlas from a Ulreth attack is this dynamic in action.

Anti-Ulreth Tactics (The Frontier Tradition)
--------------------------------------------

The core tactical reality: fighting something stronger than you, potentially in
numbers, that doesn't respond to pain or intimidation the way a human does but
behaves according to predictable territorial instincts. Over 1,300 years,
frontier communities accumulated deep behavioral knowledge about Ulreth --- when
they charge, what triggers retreat, how they coordinate, what their sensory
capabilities are.

### The Hold

The foundational frontier skill. A line of spears behind an obstacle --- fence,
ditch, fallen trees, wagon --- presenting a wall of points to an approaching
group. The technique is not about killing Ulreth. It exploits the fact that they
are instinct-driven: the behavioral threshold where territorial defense switches
to self-preservation is the entire tactical target.

Frontier people would know that threshold intimately --- how many spear-points
per yard of line, how much noise, how much visible resistance it takes to flip
the switch. The hold is boring. Standing in a line and not breaking. The single
most common way people die on the frontier is when someone breaks the line.

### Driving

Herders developed this independently of military tradition. On a mountainside,
you don't fight a Ulreth encounter the way you fight one at a settlement wall.
You redirect. Using terrain features, noise, fire, thrown stones (slings), and
coordinated movement to push Ulreth away from livestock or people without
closing to contact range.

This is what Baret uses to save Barlas --- herding instincts, terrain reading,
animal behavior applied to a combat situation. The practical application of
understanding Ulreth as animals with behavioral patterns rather than enemies
with intentions.

Key maneuvers within driving:

-   **Flanking noise.** Getting behind or beside a group to create a pressure
    gradient that redirects movement.
-   **High-ground positioning.** Ulreth, being territorial ground-dwellers,
    respond to threats from above differently than lateral threats. Mountain
    herders exploit this instinctively.
-   **Reading commitment.** Knowing when a Ulreth is posturing versus genuinely
    attacking, which determines whether you stand or redirect. Misreading this
    gets people killed.

### The Kill Stroke

When a Ulreth closes to contact range --- past the spear line, past the driving
perimeter --- the fight must end fast because a human loses a sustained grapple
against something physically stronger. This is a single committed downward or
lateral strike with a heavy blade aimed at wherever a Ulreth is structurally
vulnerable.

1,300 years of killing them means frontier people know the anatomy cold ---
major blood vessels, skeletal weak points, optimal strike angles for fastest
incapacitation. This doesn't look like swordsmanship. It looks like butchery,
because that's what it is. One efficient cut to a known target. The technique
exists because you get one shot before the thing is on top of you.

### Trap-Setting as Martial Discipline

Not thought of as a martial art, but functionally one. The construction and
maintenance of defensive perimeters is the single most effective anti-Ulreth
technique available. A good trap line kills more Ulreth than any fighter ever
will.

Skills involved:

-   **Terrain reading.** Where will they approach from? What natural features
    channel movement?
-   **Construction.** How deep a pit, what angle of stake, what trigger
    mechanism for a deadfall?
-   **Maintenance.** Traps degrade, need resetting, need to be moved when Ulreth
    learn to avoid them --- and the smarter ones do learn.

The near-human intelligence of upper-spectrum Ulreth creates an arms race. The
best trap-setters on the frontier are engaged in a slow-motion evolutionary
contest with Ulreth that can remember and adapt. This grounds the Ulreth as a
real ecological threat rather than a plot device and gives frontier communities
expertise the elite system doesn't value --- feeding the series' class themes
directly.

### Night Discipline

Rigid protocols around fire management, watch rotation, noise discipline, and
light discipline, shaped by 1,300 years of accumulated knowledge about Ulreth
behavioral patterns around the day/night cycle. The specific maneuvers are about
positioning --- where you sleep relative to the fire, how sentries are placed,
what the alarm protocol is, how you move at night if necessary. Less dramatic
than spear combat but what keeps people alive.

Anti-Human Tactics (The Feudal / Proving Tradition)
---------------------------------------------------

Built for the Provings and for enforcing Haldrin's authority. Recognizably
different from frontier combat --- individual skill rather than group
coordination, bladed weapons rather than spears, technique rather than terrain
exploitation.

### The Matched Fight

The Provings pit individuals against each other in structured bouts. Over 1,300
years, a formal combat system developed around medium-length bladed weapons ---
long knives or short swords. The tradition emphasizes reading the opponent's
movement, controlling distance, and exploiting openings.

Techniques are practical rather than acrobatic. Armor availability means the
fighting style accounts for partially armored opponents (targeting gaps and
joints) versus unarmored opponents (more options, faster pace). The Proving
format shapes what techniques get practiced, meaning the martial tradition is
partly an artifact of competition rules rather than pure combat efficiency.

### Grappling and Informal Fighting

Below the formal Proving tradition: brawls, enforcement actions, the kind of
fight Baret gets into defending Elowen at fourteen. Not systematized the way the
blade tradition is. Scrappy, regional, taught by experience rather than
instruction.

Mountain communities fight differently than lowlanders based on terrain
familiarity (fighting on slopes is a specific skill), build differences, and
local conventions about what's acceptable.

### The Disarm / Submission

In a feudal system, you sometimes need to capture rather than kill --- hostages
for ransom, subduing for the lord's justice, control without execution.
Higher-status skills (retainers need them, herders don't) representing martial
sophistication that comes from political complexity rather than survival
pressure.

Where the Two Traditions Collide
--------------------------------

The Provings test blade skill and individual combat --- the elite tradition. The
frontier produces people whose martial instincts are about group coordination,
terrain exploitation, behavioral reading, and practical lethality against
non-human threats. Baret comes from the second tradition and enters the first.

His herding background gives him terrain reading, endurance, threat assessment,
and the driving/redirection instincts that save Barlas. What it doesn't give him
is blade technique, individual combat structure, or the specific skills the
Provings test. He is competent in Proving 1 because mountain endurance and
adaptability translate partially, but he is not dominant until the AI provides
motor cortex optimization --- uploading the individual combat proficiency he was
never trained in.

### The Rebellion (Book 1, Act 3)

Baret's forces are frontier people using frontier techniques against retainers
trained in the elite tradition. Spear lines and terrain exploitation against
blade-trained individual fighters. A genuine tactical mismatch, not a guaranteed
win for either side.

### The War (Book 2)

Once the republic forms and military organization replaces feudal retainers,
professional military doctrine emerges that combines both traditions ---
organized units using spear formations and defensive engineering (frontier
knowledge) with trained individual fighters in key positions (feudal martial
tradition). Activated individuals operate within this framework as force
multipliers: scouts who process terrain faster, commanders who anticipate
movements by seconds.

The Ginak
---------

A ritualized contest from the feudal era that survived into the republic. Named
from /g/ (conflict, opposition, boundaries) --- "a struggle at the boundary."
The challenged party cannot refuse without disgrace. The duel demands public
attendance and official witness.

The ginak is the one element of the martial tradition that deserves full
development of specific rules and conventions, because it carries thematic
weight across the series. Baret built institutions of law and process
specifically to replace the feudal culture of personal combat and honor
contests. When he exploits the ginak in Book 5, he is using the old world's
customs to reclaim the new world he built --- a confession that his republic
never fully outgrew the culture it replaced.

Narrative Principles
--------------------

-   The martial culture should be revealed through characters being in the wrong
    context for their training, not through taxonomy. Show Baret using driving
    instincts in a Proving context where they don't quite fit. Show a retainer
    freezing on the frontier because his training never covered group Ulreth
    encounters.
-   Combat scenes work because they are grounded in character. The emotional
    weight comes from who these people are, not from technical details of
    fighting style.
-   Weapons and martial culture should exist as lived texture --- the spear
    Baret carries while herding, the expensive blade a retainer's child uses in
    the Provings, the trap lines around a frontier settlement --- rather than as
    an elaborate system the reader needs to learn.
-   Don't name techniques with constructed-language terms unless a specific
    technique matters to the plot. The ginak works because it is a plot-relevant
    institution.
