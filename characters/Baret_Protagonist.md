# BARET --- Protagonist

**Status:** ✅ Core arc established / ✅ Scene-level personality established

**Name:** Baret (from seed language /b/ onset --- substance category. Ba-ret,
CV-CVC. Previously considered Leofric, rejected as too ornamental; Baret
adjusted to drop non-systemic double-t)

**Age:** 17 at start of Book 1

**Background:** Working-class, from a wooded mountainous region under Lord
Haldrin's feudal domain. His family lost his sister Nela to what was reported as
a Ulreth raid when he was younger, but the family knows the truth: she was raped
by [[Kesal_Retainer|Kesal]], one of [[Haldrin_Lord|Haldrin's]] retainers, who
then covered it up by attributing her death to the Ulreth. Haldrin likely
suspects the truth but won't pursue it --- Kesal is loyal, and justice for a
dead commoner's daughter isn't worth the cost of losing a useful man. Baret
doesn't know the truth at the start of Book 1. He pieces it together after his
AI activation in Capsule 2, when enhanced cognitive processing allows him to
reread memories and see patterns that never added up --- timelines that don't
match, behavior that makes no sense as grief but makes perfect sense as a
cover-up.

**Personality:** Gruff, reserved, not emotionally perceptive. Average size but
confident enough --- he's lived on the frontier, which doesn't allow for timid
types. A bit of a slob --- leaves things lying around, doesn't keep his space
tidy, which annoys Darek to no end. Doesn't laugh much, but when he does it's a
girlish giggle that embarrasses him. Two things trigger it: a ticklish spot in
his ribs that Darek knows exactly how to find, and anyone losing their pants in
public --- straightforward slapstick that cracks him every time. Very
competitive --- loves racing his brother or anyone else whenever he gets a
chance. He has a tendency to close his eyes when he's thinking. He paces when
he's agitated. When he's nervous he rubs a religious token he carries with him
--- this sits alongside the lion's claw as his two anchoring objects (family and
faith). Driven initially by class anger and raw grief --- Nela died
approximately six months before the story begins. After the AI-assisted
discovery, his anger transforms from "the Ulreth killed my sister" to "the
system that was supposed to protect her destroyed her and lied about it." Not a
natural leader --- he stumbles into leadership through capability and principle
rather than ambition. His first act of leadership is standing up for Elowen when
she's being picked on by village youths. Values truth over safety, transparency
over control. Believes questions shouldn't be feared. Fundamentally believes in
the humanity of individuals --- that no one is inherently better or worse
because of birth.

## Scene-Level Behavior

**Conflict style:** His first approach is to go quiet. This changes after he
breaks with silence --- after which he's slow to articulate differences of
opinion, carefully analyzing alternatives before committing. But once he's
worked through it and reached a conviction, he's not afraid to speak up and
won't back down. This frustrates people who want fast answers --- the quiet gets
mistaken for weakness until they hit the wall of his conviction.

**Physical expression:** He likes to wrestle with other men even when it's not
exactly appropriate or he doesn't really know them. It's his way of showing
affection --- physical intimacy disguised as competition. A frontier kid who
can't express warmth verbally, so he grabs people instead. The comedy comes from
doing this to diplomats, religious figures, or people he's just met. He also has
a habit of rubbing the glyphs on his palms because they occasionally tingle ---
a sign of strong conduit connection to the symbiont system. In religious circles
this is interpreted as spiritual blessing: Baret is seen as divinely favored.
The irony is that what the church calls a gift from the divine is actually a
genetic lottery result from 1,300-year-old bioengineering. The tingling is a
biological signal about his neural interface quality, not a mark of holiness.
This detail reads as texture in Book 1 (a kid rubbing his palms), becomes a
worldbuilding seed in Book 3 (when Baret learns what the symbionts actually are
and remembers every time someone called his tingling palms a blessing), and
functions as evidence on reread.

**Speech pattern:** He repeats obscure country wisdom --- mountain proverbs and
frontier sayings that don't make much sense or confuse people who aren't
familiar with them. This gives him a distinctive voice that no other character
shares, and marks him permanently as a frontier kid even in rooms built for
courtiers. Over five books, the reader and his inner circle learn to decode his
proverbs, creating intimacy with the character.

## Constructive Goal

He wants a world where people can live without fear --- where anyone has the
opportunity to grow regardless of who they were born to. This is abstract at 17
and will need to crystallize into concrete policy and institution-building
across the series, but the emotional core is there from the start: he grew up
watching his parents practice quiet, subtle resistance against an oppressive
system they couldn't openly challenge, and he wants to build something that
makes that kind of silent endurance unnecessary.

## Family Context

His parents suspect the truth about Nela's death but can't prove anything under
the oppressive feudal system. Their stance is quiet and subtle resistance ---
enduring, protecting what they have, resisting where they can without being
crushed. Baret's revolution is the loud version of what his parents were already
doing quietly.

## Core Arc Across Five Books

**Book 1:** Enters the trials carrying raw grief --- Nela died six months ago.
First act of leadership: defending Elowen from village youths, which unknowingly
begins the romantic thread. Class anger transforms into targeted fury when AI
activation lets him piece together the truth about Nela's death. Survives the
trials, overthrows the lord. Learns that revolution is an explosion --- you
don't choose where the shrapnel goes. Falls for Arabela, overlooks Elowen.

**Book 2:** War leader, first real use of AI symbiont as tactical tool. Rallying
speech becomes his greatest leadership moment AND directly produces conditions
for the lord's murder. Pulls away for ambiguous beast race encounter, creating a
power vacuum.

**Book 3:** Allies with the religious leader, accesses Capsule 1, discovers
partial truth about human origins. Breaks into restricted archives. Carries the
secret --- his first genuine moral compromise. Confronts the oligarch and fails
to change him through persuasion. Faith erodes gradually.

**Book 4:** Secret detonates, breaks with religious leader. Builds imperfect
republican institutions. Brother's apparent betrayal (actually Kullen's
manipulation) forces him into exile. Elowen returns the geode at his lowest
point --- the moment he finally sees her. Develops moral courage and emotional
maturity.

**Book 5:** Investigates institutional decay, discovers it's deliberate sabotage
via memory manipulation. Realizes brother was a victim, not a traitor ---
compounded by years of resentment he now regrets. Confronts Kullen. Secures the
immediate threat but lives with the knowledge that the vulnerability is
permanent.

## What He Carries

A lion's claw from when his father saved him from one as a child. It's his
anchor to what it means to protect those who are weak and vulnerable --- not as
an abstraction, but as a lived memory of someone putting themselves between
danger and someone they loved. The claw gains tragic weight once the truth
emerges: his father could protect him from a lion but couldn't protect Nela from
the system that was supposed to protect all of them.

He also carries a religious token that he rubs when nervous --- this sits
alongside the lion's claw as his two anchoring objects (family and faith). \##
Strength

He sees systems. Where other people see a village, a battle, a political
alliance, Baret sees the structure underneath --- who has power, where the
pressure points are, what happens if you pull this thread. This is why he can
overthrow a lord at 17 and build a republic by his thirties. He's a structural
thinker.

## Physical Capability

Baret is a warrior. Every child in his mountain territory grows up familiar with
weapons, terrain navigation, animal handling, and Ulreth awareness --- baseline
martial competence is universal on the frontier. Before activation, Baret is a
good fighter among many good fighters: confident, physically capable, nothing
exceptional. After activation, his motor cortex enhancement transforms his
combat ability --- not by adding strength or mass, but by improving
coordination, reaction speed, and movement efficiency. He reads opponents
faster, exploits openings instinctively, and moves with precision that trained
fighters recognize as dangerous without understanding why.

**Book 1:** Martially competent but unremarkable pre-activation. Post-activation
motor cortex calibration makes him noticeably better during the later Provings,
but he's still 16--17 and physically immature. Advantages are tactical and
technical, not dominant.

**Book 2:** War sharpens him. Two years of real combat against human armies and
Ulreth, guided by an AI optimizing his motor patterns in real time. By the end
of Book 2, Baret is one of the more formidable individual fighters on the
continent --- not because of superior strength, but because he fought a war with
a computer in his head refining every movement.

**Book 3:** Fighting reputation established but role has shifted to leadership
and politics. Physical danger comes from covert operations --- the Capsule 1
infiltration, navigating Tobas's controlled environment, surviving assassination
attempts and pursuit. Combat is situational and desperate rather than
ritualized.

**Book 4:** The escape costs him physically. He fights his way out of a system
that turned on him. The accumulated damage --- burns, blade scars, injuries that
change his appearance --- is significant enough that years later, in a dim
fighting pit, he's not immediately recognizable. The scarring carries narrative
weight: the story written on his body from the moment his alliance with Tobas
shattered.

**Book 5:** Exile has given him years to train without political obligations.
His AI has been optimizing motor patterns for over a decade. Physically in his
prime at 30--32, frontier-hardened, scarred. He enters the underground fighting
circuit as someone who is genuinely, quietly dangerous --- and the tension isn't
whether Baret can win fights but whether winning will expose him.

## Weakness

Multiple, layered, and designed to evolve across the series. He's slow to see
individuals --- chases Arabela while overlooking Elowen, misjudges Darek for
years. His commitment to principle shades into rigidity. His revolution produced
an extrajudicial killing he couldn't prevent, and that haunts his governance. He
counts people before he knows them. These are not flaws he overcomes cleanly ---
they're tensions he manages with increasing self-awareness.

## Symbiont/AI [[Baret_Symbiot]]

Fragmentary AI from Capsule 2 activation. Cognitive and motor cortex
enhancement. AI is a collaborator with limitations, corrupted data, and blind
spots --- not an oracle. Designed for tactical and systems analysis, not social
intelligence --- it can identify structural weaknesses in a fortification but
can't read body language or emotional subtext. This is a design limitation, not
a gap: the capsule designers were building soldiers and workers, not emotionally
intelligent companions. This is why the AI never flags Elowen's feelings for
Baret --- it's outside its operational domain, and Baret never thinks to ask a
military system about emotions. Hippocampal degradation noted as a clinical
footnote (this becomes critical in Book 5 when it's revealed the degradation
makes some people susceptible to memory manipulation).

## Key Relationships

**Darek:** Paternal twin. Strong, familiar, complementary. Playful banter that
sometimes escalates into real fights. Darek picks at Baret when Baret wants to
be left alone. Baret's slobbiness drives the fastidious Darek crazy. They race
each other constantly. Darek criticizes everyone's cooking and tries to feed
Baret when he's brooding; Baret confuses everyone with mountain proverbs while
Darek translates for the room. The rib-poking is the signature of their
closeness --- Darek knows exactly where to hit to get the giggle. Darek is the
only person never intimidated by Baret --- he bursts Baret's bubble whenever
Baret gets high-minded, keeping him grounded. This warmth is what makes the Book
4 fracture devastating and the Book 5 revelation unbearable.

**Kullen:** They meet at the trials in Book 1 --- Kullen is the lord's son with
every advantage, Baret is the commoner who shouldn't be a threat. Baret
initially finds Kullen stuck up and associates him with Haldrin's system ---
blames the son for the father's world. Over the series, Baret comes to see
Kullen as an individual, a product of the same system from a different angle.
There should be a moment later in the series where the two connect intimately
--- a recognition that if they'd been born in the same class, they might have
been real friends. The thing that breaks this connection is the difference in
values: Kullen believes to the end that there are classes of people, some better
than others, and feels no remorse for exploiting that hierarchy. Baret
fundamentally believes in the humanity of individuals. The tragedy is that the
understanding is real but the gulf is unbridgeable.

**Elowen:** He defends her when she's being picked on after her family's
disgrace, gives her a pretty geode because it's in his pocket and she's upset.
He doesn't think about it again. She keeps it for years. He doesn't see her
clearly until Book 4 when everything else has fallen apart. They share the same
goal --- a world without the class system that destroyed both their lives ---
but arrive at it from opposite directions. His drive is anger over Nela. Hers is
sadness over the servant girl. Same revolution, different fuel.

## What's Missing

- The constructive goal needs to crystallize from abstract ("world without
  fear") into something concrete and scene-level as the series progresses
- Does his father know the truth too, or only his mother? How does the secret
  sit between the parents?
- The religious token --- what does it look like? Does he stop rubbing it after
  the break with Tobas in Book 4, or does the habit persist even after the
  belief is gone?
- Food, drink, sleep habits --- the daily-life texture that fills scenes between
  major beats
- What does he do when he's alone? - Book 4 scarring specifics --- what is the
  specific moment or fight that produces the facial/body scarring? A named
  opponent, a fire during the escape, Kullen's people catching him? The origin
  matters because the scars carry narrative weight in every Book 5 scene where
  someone looks at his face
- Underground fighting entry mechanism in Book 5 --- does Barlas pull him in, or
  does he enter independently? If Barlas is a fixture in the circuit, the
  reunion carries weight: Barlas built a life in the space the revolution
  created and abandoned
