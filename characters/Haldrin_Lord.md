**LORD HALDRIN --- The Feudal Lord**
------------------------------------

**Status:** ⚠️ Function clear / ❌ Scene-level character missing

**Name:** Haldrin (from seed language /h/ onset --- governance/abstract
category. Hal-drin, CVC-CVC. The /dr/ cluster is a drift artifact from a lost
vowel. Added initial H to fix vowel-onset violation)

**Background:** Feudal lord of Baret's region. Runs violent trials to sort
genetic capability among subjects. Genuinely believes consolidating power and
sacrificing outlying villages is the only way to survive the Ulreth. Loves his
family while being a ruthless ruler.

**Personality:** A hard man. Principled --- but his principles are brutal.
Unwavering and stubborn beyond reason. He doesn't bend, doesn't negotiate,
doesn't reconsider. Once he's decided something is necessary, it's done
regardless of the cost. This isn't cruelty for its own sake --- it's conviction
so deep it becomes indistinguishable from cruelty. He's the kind of ruler who
believes compromise is weakness and mercy is a luxury the frontier can't afford.
A loving father and a ruthless lord in the same breath.

**Core Arc:**

-   **Book 1:** Primary antagonist. His system of brutal triage --- deciding who
    is expendable --- is what Baret fights against. He's not evil; he's solving
    a different problem. Surrenders after Baret's rebellion. Murdered by Baret's
    companion after surrender.

**What He Carries:** His battle standard. It goes with him everywhere --- to
councils, to inspections, to meals. It's the symbol of what his family has held
and what it cost to hold it. He thinks in terms of soldiers --- every decision
is measured against the lives it costs to defend the land. Not in an abstract
way. He knows the numbers. He remembers the names.

**Strength:** He's actually good at the job. Under Haldrin's rule, the Ulreth
kill fewer people than they would under anyone else in the region. His brutal
triage works. The villages he sacrifices buy time for the villages he protects.
The trials identify the most capable defenders. He's monstrous *and effective*,
which is why people tolerate him and why the reader should be uncomfortable.

**Weakness:** He can't conceive of a system that doesn't require sacrifice. He's
so locked into triage-thinking --- counting soldiers, measuring costs --- that
he genuinely cannot imagine a world where you don't have to choose who dies.
When Baret proposes that alternative, Haldrin doesn't fight it out of
self-interest --- he fights it because he thinks Baret is going to get everyone
killed through naivety. He's not wrong about the risk. He's wrong about the
impossibility.
