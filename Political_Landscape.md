# Political Landscape of Alinar

**Reference Document --- Against the Grade Series** **Timeline: \~1300 years
post-landing \| Population: \~7 million**

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Overview

Human civilization on Alinar grew from a single point of origin --- Capsule 1
(Paksut) in the mountainous northwest of the central continent. Over 1300 years,
population expanded primarily eastward and southward along river valleys,
constrained by mountain ranges, coastlines, and the persistent Ulreth threat
from the northeast. Settled human territory spans roughly 2000 km from the
origin point, leaving the eastern third of the continent and the deep south as
Ulreth-held or unclaimed wilderness.

The political landscape at the start of the series is the product of at least
two complete cycles of consolidation and fragmentation --- a golden age kingdom
that unified much of the settled territory roughly 400--800 years post-landing,
followed by its collapse into the present patchwork of competing polities. Every
faction carries grievances rooted in that history, and every border reflects a
geographic barrier that outlasted the political arrangements around it.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## The Biological Foundation of Political Power

Political power on Alinar cannot be understood separately from the symbiont
system. All humans carry a biological second brain --- the symbiont ---
engineered into their DNA. It was designed to remain inert until an AI assistant
was uploaded to it. After \~1300 years of genetic drift under roughly 8× Earth
radiation, the population breaks down approximately as follows:

- **\~15--25% functional:** Symbiont architecture intact enough to accept
  activation if infrastructure existed. These bloodlines form the basis of the
  aristocratic class, though most have never been activated and don't know they
  could be.
- **\~20--30% partially degraded:** Some architecture preserved, unpredictable
  activation potential. The source of the "Trials" religious tradition ---
  people who believe discipline and effort can awaken dormant capability.
- **\~45--60% non-functional:** Architecture too degraded by accumulated
  mutations. No activation possible regardless of access to infrastructure.

This distribution is invisible to the population. Nobody understands the
biology. What they see is that some bloodlines consistently produce stronger,
faster, more capable people --- and they've built political, religious, and
economic systems to explain and exploit that observation. The feudal aristocracy
justifies itself through demonstrated capability that is, at root, a genetic
lottery nobody chose.

The mountain communities where Baret originates show a distinct founder-effect
pattern: degraded hippocampal pathways that make their symbionts partially
resistant to memory manipulation. This is the genetic accident that ultimately
makes Baret immune to Kullen's military AI --- but at the start of the series,
it manifests only as a subtle cognitive difference nobody has named.

## The Territories

### Sacred Territory (\~44,000 km², \~400,000 people)

**Geography:** The mountainous northwest around Capsule 1 (Paksut/Hakundal).
High terrain, tight valleys, limited agricultural capacity. The oldest
continuously settled area on the planet.

**Character:** A small territory with outsized influence. The Interrupted
Promise faith is headquartered here, built literally on and around Capsule 1's
ruins --- walls of alien material repurposed as ceremonial spaces, objects of
unknown function venerated as divine gifts. The orthodoxy controls access to the
most sacred site in human civilization, which gives them disproportionate
political leverage despite their small population.

**Power base:** Ideological authority. The Sacred Territory functions like a
medieval Papal State --- its power is soft rather than military, operating
through moral legitimacy, pilgrimage traffic, and the ability to declare
positions righteous or heretical. Centuries of accumulated wealth from religious
donations and land grants make the religious establishment financially
independent.

**Internal tensions:** The orthodox establishment maintains the doctrine that
marks are evidence of divine promise --- not proof of worthiness, but reminders
that the original design persists. This egalitarian theology is genuine and was
historically progressive. By the time of the story, however, the institution has
calcified around protecting its own authority. Questions about what the sacred
site actually *is* threaten the entire framework, which is why Tobas's evolution
from ally to antagonist feels inevitable.

**Key locations:** Paksut (Capsule 1, common name), Hakundal (sacred name for
the same site), Pakur (Baret's mountain village, \~300 people).

**Relationship to Baret:** This is his cultural formation. He grows up steeped
in the religion's gravity even though his mountain community has a practical
Trials bent. His faith is real at the start of the series --- not performative,
not superficial.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Crown Lands (\~182,000 km², \~1,500,000 people)

**Geography:** The north-central lowlands controlling the major river
convergence. The most fertile agricultural land in the settled territories, with
the best natural transportation routes.

**Character:** The historical political center and the seat of feudal power.
Whoever controls where the major rivers meet controls grain distribution and
movement. The Crown Lands represent the old establishment --- the lineage that
consolidated power during the middle period and has been slowly losing effective
authority ever since.

**Power base:** Institutional legitimacy and geographic advantage. The Crown
Seat at Punsak sits at the river convergence. The feudal aristocracy here holds
power through land ownership, legal tradition, and the memory of past
unification. But the aristocracy is increasingly hollow --- many noble families
have visible but non-functional symbiont marks, performing a status their
biology no longer supports.

**Internal tensions:** The merchant class in Punsak and Punasir has real wealth
but no political standing under the feudal system. They need legal frameworks
that protect commerce rather than hereditary privilege. They're physically
concentrated enough to organize collectively. These are the people who will form
the backbone of republican institutions when Baret's revolution dismantles the
feudal order --- not because they share his idealism, but because republics are
better for business.

An administrative class of clerks, magistrates, and record-keepers already runs
the actual machinery of governance. They don't fit the feudal
warrior-aristocrat/peasant binary. They'll become the bureaucratic
infrastructure of whatever replaces feudalism, because they already know how to
run things.

**Key locations:** Punsak (Crown Seat, capital), Punasir (river city, major
trade hub).

**Relationship to Baret:** The seat of the power structure he eventually
overthrows. Haldrin (the feudal lord of Baret's territory) answers to the Crown,
and the Crown's relationship to its peripheral vassals --- taxing them while
underinvesting in their defense --- is part of what makes the frontier
communities resentful.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Northern Reach (\~534,000 km², \~600,000 people)

**Geography:** The broad coastal territory north and east of the Crown Lands,
stretching along the northern coast of the continent. Mixes lowland coastal
areas with the rising terrain approaching the Ulreth frontier.

**Character:** A nominally governed territory that is too large for its
population to control effectively. At \~4 people per km², this is frontier land
--- settlements concentrated along rivers and the coast with vast stretches of
unsettled territory between them. The Crown claims it. In practice, local lords
govern their own small domains and acknowledge the Crown when it suits them.

**Power base:** Scattered. No single power center. This territory exists because
the geography between the Crown Lands and the Frontier Marches doesn't present a
clean natural border. It's the kind of territory that would fragment further
under political pressure --- or be absorbed by a stronger neighbor during a
crisis.

**Internal tensions:** Coastal communities with maritime connections feel little
loyalty to highland lords. Communities near the Ulreth frontier share more
culturally with the Frontier Marches than with the Crown. The territory's
coherence depends on nobody testing it.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Frontier Marches (\~176,000 km², \~600,000 people)

**Geography:** The northeastern buffer zone between human settlement and Ulreth
territory. Terrain transitions from settled lowlands to the contested highlands
approaching the NE land bridge.

**Character:** A permanently militarized society. The Ulreth flow continuously
through the land bridge from the northeast, which means the Frontier Marches
exist in a state of constant military readiness. The Marcher Lords are the most
capable military commanders in human civilization because failure means Ulreth
incursions into the interior. This is the shield of settled Alinar, and everyone
who lives here knows it.

**Power base:** Military capability and geographic necessity. The Marcher Lords
are simultaneously the Crown's most essential vassals and the most resentful.
They bleed for a kingdom that taxes them, underinvests in their defense, and
treats them as provincial. Their political leverage is existential --- if they
stop defending, everyone behind them suffers.

**Internal tensions:** The strongest Trials tradition exists here, because marks
that *work* keep you alive on the frontier. There's quiet tolerance of the
Kinship heresy among soldiers who've observed Ulreth intelligence up close and
developed a more nuanced understanding than the interior's mythology allows. The
frontier produces pragmatists who see the interior's theological and
aristocratic squabbles as luxuries afforded by people who've never watched a
Ulreth raid.

**Key locations:** Pakdan (frontier fortress), Gulborder Outpost (forward
position near Ulreth territory).

**Relationship to Baret:** His natural ally pool --- and also where Haldrin
draws his legitimacy. The lord's insistence on Ulreth defense isn't tyranny;
it's the entire reason his position exists. Baret's early struggle is against
someone doing a necessary job badly, not against a pure villain.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Southern Heartland (\~179,000 km², \~2,800,000 people)

**Geography:** The river valleys south of the central mountain spine (Takdal
Mountains). The most extensive arable land in the settled territories, fed by
multiple river systems draining southward from the mountains.

**Character:** The demographic center of gravity. This is where the bulk of the
population lives because this is where the food grows. The territory expanded
gradually as population pressure in the north pushed settlers southward through
mountain passes. By the time of the story, it has more people than every other
territory combined --- but political power still sits in the Crown Lands to the
north, where institutions were established centuries before the south was fully
settled.

**Power base:** Food production and population. The Southern Heartland feeds the
continent. This gives it enormous latent political power that the feudal system
has never acknowledged. The southern communities were founded by people who
*left* the established territories --- often unmarked or low-status people
seeking better prospects. The founding mythology here isn't about the sacred
site; it's about making something new.

**Internal tensions:** The south's patience with northern governance has a shelf
life. This is the kindling for the feudalism-to-republic transition. The south
has the people and the food; the north has the tradition and the institutions.
The Egalitarian tradition of the Interrupted Promise is strongest here,
concentrated among unmarked merchant families wealthy enough to resent being
told their spiritual status is lesser. Their theology undermines hereditary
privilege even when they don't frame it politically.

**Key locations:** Pundis (plains city, agricultural center), Taksir (southern
port/town).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Trade Corridor (\~343,000 km², \~800,000 people)

**Geography:** The southeastern region controlling routes between the Southern
Heartland and the eastern coast. Every trade route between the agricultural
south and the northern or eastern markets passes through this corridor.

**Character:** The oligarch's power base, and the geography explains why.
Families who controlled these routes for generations accumulated wealth
independent of feudal land grants. Economic power eventually became political
power. Murek isn't self-made --- he's the inheritor of a system that's been
concentrating commercial wealth in this corridor for centuries. He represents a
class, not just a person.

**Power base:** Control of movement. The Trade Corridor doesn't produce much ---
it facilitates exchange. This makes it the most cosmopolitan territory (trade
hubs attract everyone), the most pragmatic about symbiont status (money doesn't
care about your mark), and the most vulnerable to disruption (any conflict that
blocks the routes threatens the entire economic model).

**Internal tensions:** The eastern edge of the Trade Corridor is exposed to
Ulreth flanking pressure along the coast, giving the oligarch legitimate
military concerns he can leverage for political influence. The merchant families
who run this territory want stability above all else --- they'll support whoever
keeps the routes open and oppose whoever threatens them, regardless of ideology.

**Key locations:** Pasnal (trade port, the corridor's largest settlement).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Western Peninsula (\~333,000 km², \~200,000 people)

**Geography:** The isolated western extension of the continent, connected to the
mainland by narrow coastal terrain. Naturally cut off from the political center
by water and mountains.

**Character:** A geographic afterthought that developed independently. Maritime
culture, fishing and coastal trade economy, loose confederation of communities.
Too small to threaten anyone, too isolated to conquer easily, not worth the
military investment to subjugate.

**Power base:** Independence through irrelevance. The Western Peninsula pays
nominal tribute to the Crown but is functionally self-governing. It survives by
being not worth the trouble of controlling.

**Narrative function:** A place characters can flee to, trade through, or
recruit from outside the main power structures. Potentially the strongest naval
tradition on the continent, which could matter if anyone ever needs to move
forces by sea.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Deep South (\~170,000 km², \~300,000 people)

**Geography:** The far southern mountains and coastal lowlands. Rugged terrain,
sparse settlement, long travel times to anywhere that matters politically.

**Character:** Where people go when they've been pushed out of everywhere else
--- or when they want to disappear. Escaped serfs, religious dissidents,
political exiles. No unified governance, just scattered highland communities and
coastal settlements.

**Power base:** None. The Deep South has no political coherence. It's the
frontier in the truest sense --- territory being settled for the first time,
with all the lawlessness and independence that implies.

**Narrative function:** The wilderness where Capsule 3 hides. This is the kind
of empty, unmapped territory where a dispossessed lord's son like Kullen drifts
because nobody with better options goes there. His discovery of Capsule 3 in a
drained mountain lake is plausible precisely because the Deep South is the part
of the continent nobody has thoroughly explored.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Ulreth Territory (\~1,243,000 km²)

**Geography:** The entire eastern third of the continent, centered on the NE
land bridge and extending south along the eastern coast. The largest single zone
on the map. This represents the area where Ulreth population density and
territorial behavior make sustained human settlement impossible.

**Character:** Not a political territory in the human sense. The Ulreth are
near-sapient territorial organisms --- unfinished drafts from Capsule 2's rushed
biological sequence. They lack centralized organization, agriculture, or
permanent settlements. What they have is numbers, territorial instinct, adaptive
behavior, and a degraded ecosystem around their origin point (Capsule 2 /
Gulpak) that humans can't farm.

**The frontier dynamic:** The Ulreth don't invade; they *press*. Population
growth pushes them westward through the land bridge and along the coast. Border
raids hit frontier settlements, take inorganic resources (metal, tools, water),
and retreat. Human military expeditions push them back temporarily, but there's
no capital to capture and no army to defeat. The Ulreth are dispersed across a
massive area of territory that humans can't occupy. Every campaign is a
logistics nightmare with no endpoint.

**Why 7 million humans haven't exterminated them:** The degraded ecosystem
around Capsule 2 prevents human settlement. You can send armies in, but you
can't hold ground because you can't feed an occupying force off the land.
Capsule 2's contamination legacy --- predators too aggressive, herbivores with
wrong metabolic rates, disrupted pollination and food webs --- makes the inner
Ulreth territory economically worthless and logistically brutal to operate in.
The Ulreth survive not because they're strong enough to resist humanity, but
because their homeland resists human settlement.

**What the interior doesn't understand:** Frontier communities who've observed
Ulreth for generations have noticed the intelligence, the tool use, the social
structures. The Kinship heresy --- the belief that the Ulreth share a biological
origin with humans --- is suppressed by the orthodox establishment because it
threatens the foundational myth of the Interrupted Promise. The heresy is,
ironically, closer to the truth than any other theology on the planet.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Unclaimed Wilderness (\~1,276,000 km²)

The southeastern interior and deep southern mountains. Land nobody has claimed
because nobody has reached it in numbers sufficient to hold it. This wilderness
forms a buffer between the easternmost human settlements and the Ulreth flanking
territory along the coast. As human population grows and the Ulreth continue
pressing westward, this buffer will shrink --- setting up the long-term
civilizational collision that drives the series' political arc.

## The Religious Landscape

The Interrupted Promise is the dominant faith across all human territories, but
after 1300 years of questioning, three heterodox traditions have developed
alongside the orthodoxy.

**The Orthodox tradition** maintains that the metal mothers began something that
was interrupted by violence (the Ulreth, mythologized as chaos-beasts). The
marks are evidence of the divine promise --- given without regard to merit,
proof that the original design belongs to all humans. The sacred site
(Paksut/Hakundal) is where humanity began and where the interrupted teaching
will someday resume. Moral law derives from the fragments of the original
curriculum. Strongest in the Sacred Territory and among established communities
everywhere.

**The Egalitarian tradition** rejects the spiritual significance of marks
entirely. Everyone is equally incomplete; the mark is biology, not blessing.
Strongest in urban centers and among unmarked merchant families in the Southern
Heartland. Politically aligned with republican movements even when practitioners
don't frame it that way --- their theology undermines hereditary privilege at a
fundamental level.

**The Trials tradition** believes marks can be awakened through discipline and
rigorous self-improvement. Almost certainly wrong about the mechanism, but the
belief produces a culture of demanding self-improvement that makes Trials
communities disproportionately effective as soldiers and craftspeople. Strongest
in frontier and mountain communities where practical capability matters more
than bloodline. The feudal lord in Book 1 draws his most capable fighters from
Trials communities.

**The Kinship heresy** holds that the Ulreth are also children of the metal
mothers --- incomplete, like humanity, but kin rather than monsters. The most
politically dangerous tradition and the most suppressed. Survives in border
settlements where pragmatic coexistence with Ulreth raiding is daily reality.
The orthodox establishment treats it as existentially threatening because it
challenges the foundational myth of the Ulreth as Interrupters.

The religious schism between hardliner and moderate factions within the
orthodoxy is left unresolved at series end. The "chaos harbinger" theological
framing allows hardliners to be sincerely motivated --- they genuinely believe
that questioning the faith's foundations invites the same destruction that broke
the original promise. This is not cynicism; it's fear dressed as conviction.

## The Fault Lines

The political landscape is shaped by five overlapping fault lines that no single
faction can resolve.

**Sacred Territory vs. Crown Lands:** Spiritual authority vs. secular authority.
The Crown claims to rule by right; the religious establishment claims to define
what "right" means. This tension has been simmering for centuries and shapes the
alliance-to-rupture arc between Baret and Tobas across Books 2--4.

**Crown Lands vs. Southern Heartland:** Old power vs. new demographic reality.
The north governed when it was the population center. It's not anymore. The
south's patience with northern rule has a shelf life, and that shelf life is
running out at the start of the series. This is the pressure that makes
republican institutions plausible --- the south has the people, the north has
the tradition, and neither can function without the other.

**Frontier Marches vs. Everyone:** The frontier subsidizes everyone else's
safety and resents it. The Marcher Lords have legitimate grievances, real
military capability, and a culture that values competence over bloodline. They
are the most dangerous faction if they ever stop pointing their swords eastward.
When Baret's revolution pulls military resources from frontier defense in Book
2, the Ulreth exploit the gap --- and the protagonist's family pays the price.

**Merchants vs. Aristocracy:** Within every territory, the tension between
land-based feudal power and commerce-based economic power is intensifying. The
merchants have wealth but no political standing; the aristocrats have standing
but increasingly hollow biological credentials. This tension drives the
republic's formation and shapes its eventual vulnerability to populist capture.

**The Ulreth Pressure:** Not a faction in the human political sense, but the
constant external threat shapes everything. It's the reason the frontier exists,
the justification the Crown uses for centralized authority, and the external
pressure that prevents internal conflicts from fully resolving. If the Ulreth
pressure ever stopped, the entire political equilibrium would collapse within a
generation because the only thing keeping the Marcher Lords loyal is the need
for Crown resources against a common enemy.

## Population Density Context

Medieval England at its peak (\~1300 CE) had 4--6 million people across
\~130,000 km² --- roughly 30--45 people per km². That density supported a
functioning centralized state with tax collection, courts, a road network, and
military projection to every corner.

The human territories of Alinar have \~7 million across \~1,486,000 km² of
claimed territory --- roughly 4.7 people per km² overall. But this is misleading
because population clusters heavily along river valleys and in the Southern
Heartland. Effective density in the settled core (the Crown Lands, southern
river valleys, and trade routes) is closer to 15--25 people per km², while
peripheral territories run at 1--3 people per km².

This means the political system functions at roughly late-Anglo-Saxon to
early-Norman levels of administrative complexity in the core, with frontier
conditions everywhere else. A feudal king's effective authority extends about
2--4 weeks of travel from the capital --- roughly 700--1200 km along road
networks. Beyond that distance, local lords make their own decisions, and the
Crown's authority becomes nominal. This is why the Frontier Marches, the Western
Peninsula, and the Deep South are effectively independent despite nominal fealty
to the Crown.

## What This Map Means for the Series

The political landscape is designed so that Baret's journey across it mirrors
the series' thematic progression. He begins in the geographic and cultural
margin --- a mountain village in the Sacred Territory, far from power, steeped
in a faith he hasn't yet examined. His revolution dismantles the local feudal
structure (Book 1) and creates a vacuum the broader political landscape rushes
to fill (Book 2). His alliances pull him into the Sacred Territory's
institutional power (Book 3), through the oligarch's economic network (Books
3--4), and into the republic's political arena (Book 4) before exile pushes him
to the geographic and political periphery again (Book 5).

The continent's size and the Ulreth's presence ensure that no single political
vision can ever fully succeed. The territory is too large for centralized
control, the threats are too persistent for isolationism, and the biological
inequality is too embedded for any system --- feudal, religious, republican, or
authoritarian --- to resolve. The question "does freedom require equality?"
doesn't have an answer on Alinar. It has competing answers, each with territory,
armies, and sincere believers behind them.
