**Against the Grade --- Book 1: Birthright**

**Chapter 2: Tanapun**

Detailed Scene Outline v3

**Chapter Overview**

**POV:** Baret, close third *\[POV/person still open\]*

**Time:** Late afternoon through evening, same day as Chapter 1

**Setting:** Tanapun --- fortified mountain village, \~300 people

**Emotional Register:** Homecoming warmth → grinding normalcy → casual
entitlement (food theft) → witnessed cruelty → impotent anger → communal
celebration (joy and dread in the same room) → escort news lands publicly

**Purpose:** Baret's world at ground level. First instance of witnessed
injustice. The system's cruelty made personal. The village feast celebrates
children it may never see again. Kesal's escort role announced publicly.

**Establishes:** The occupation inside Tanapun's walls, collaborator/original
divide, Hangus as local sheriff, Kesal as predatory retainer with armed men,
Maia's disproportionate fear, Baret's first inaction, Darek's externalized
anger, the village feast as communal ritual, the family in public context (Maia,
Danik, the twins), Nalin's absence felt in the feast, Kesal as escort announced
during the feast.

**Village Demographics**

**Population:** \~300 people

**Children under 18:** \~105--135 (35--45% of a pre-industrial frontier
population)

**Per birth cohort:** \~6--7 born, thinned by frontier mortality (Ulreth raids,
illness, accidents)

**Trial-age survivors (16):** \~4--6 candidates from Tanapun

This number matters. The village is not sending a faceless crowd of teenagers.
It is sending four to six specific children that every person in the village has
known since birth. Every adult at the feast has watched these children grow up.
Some of those adults lost children from earlier cohorts who went to the trials
and didn't come back. Across \~12 villages sending similar numbers, the
territory produces the 40--60 candidate cohort noted in the master outline.

**Escalation from Chapter 1:** The mountain was dangerous but navigable ---
natural threats Baret has tools for. Tanapun is oppressive and Baret has no
tools for it. Natural threats become human threats. Competence becomes
irrelevance. The feast adds a layer: the village celebrates children it may
lose, and the predator who just harassed a girl will escort them.

**Locked Decisions:** Kesal stationed nearby with overlapping jurisdiction. He
arrives on patrol with armed men. Same day as Chapter 1. Girl safe by accident,
not justice. Hangus does not intervene. Kesal steals food from stall. 4--6
candidates from Tanapun. Village feast the night before departure. Kesal escort
news arrives during the feast. Blessing ceremony with mantra opens Chapter 3.

**Reader Hooks**

**Tension hook:** A predator with armed men in a small village. Nobody acts. The
reader wants someone to act.

**Emotional hook:** The feast. Three hundred people celebrating four to six
children they've known since birth. Joy and dread. Some adults here lost
children from previous trial years.

**Mystery hook (deepening):** Maia's reaction is wrong --- at the Kesal scene
and again when the escort news lands at the feast. Something is off about this
mother's fear.

**Forward hook:** Kesal is the escort. The blessing is at dawn. The predator
becomes the guardian tomorrow.

**Scene Detail**

**Tanapun from inside the walls.** First time inside the fortification. Stone
walls, timber palisade --- functional, not decorative. Guard positions, gate
that closes at night, sight lines toward the forest edge.

**Social geography.** Collaborator families: closer to the gate,
better-maintained. Original families: further back, smaller, older stone.
Baret's family is original. Architecture as politics.

**Hangus.** Local sheriff. Part of the scenery. Not cruel by nature --- an
administrator of cruelty. Sustainable, which is worse.

**Kesal's patrol visible.** Horses near the gate. Armed men moving through or
stationed at points. Patrols come through regularly. Kesal has jurisdiction over
this cluster of villages. Unwelcome but unremarkable.

**Feast preparations.** The village is preparing for tonight. Food being
gathered, tables or benches set up in a communal space. The reader registers:
something is happening tonight. The preparations tell a story --- this is
routine, annual, a thing the village does. But the scale of preparation for a
village of 300 people says it matters.

**Mountain unease carried in.** The being-followed tension from Chapter 1's
descent doesn't vanish at the gate. Continuous transition from mountain-danger
to village-danger.

**Darek's transition.** More social inside the walls. Calls out to people,
offers someone a morel from his pocket. Food as connection. Baret quieter inside
the walls than on the mountain.

*Value shift: Normalcy → Casual entitlement → Witnessed cruelty → Impotence*

**Scene Detail**

**The food theft first.** Kesal takes food from a stall. Eats it walking.
Doesn't pay. Doesn't acknowledge. Stall owner says nothing. His men in the
vicinity. The reader sees two acts of entitlement in sequence: property then
person. Both treated the same way --- things he's entitled to because nobody
stops him. The food theft establishes the pattern before the stakes rise.

**Kesal.** The reader's first direct encounter with Nalin's killer, though
neither Baret nor the reader knows it. Jurisdiction over this cluster of
villages. Outranks Hangus. His patrol --- armed men --- is in the village.

**Do not make him monstrous.** Ordinary. Smooth, comfortable, entitled. Touches
things that aren't his because nobody has ever stopped him. The food theft and
the harassment are the same gesture at different scales. The banality makes it
systemic.

**The armed men.** They change everything. Nobody creates a confrontation with
soldiers ten paces away. Even the accidental rescue has to be a social maneuver
--- "Come help me with this" --- not a challenge.

**The girl.** Someone the village knows. A neighbor's daughter, someone Baret
and Darek know by name. She's frozen --- not weakness, but the knowledge that
resistance escalates and soldiers are ten paces away. She needs a name and
enough specificity that the reader remembers a person.

**Baret's inaction.** He hesitates. Something resists --- the same instinct that
read terrain and positioned himself protectively on the descent. His body knows
what it wants to do. But this is not a mountain. He shuts down --- goes still,
inward. Same pattern as the sister grief in Chapter 1.

**Darek's reaction.** Angry. Visibly. Jaw tight, something muttered. But doesn't
act. People-pleaser pattern --- feels it, shows it, doesn't confront.

**Maia.** Urgency when she pulls Baret away. Specificity of fear. She may not
say Kesal's name. Her body knows him.

On first read: cautious mother before the Marking.

On reread: watching her daughter's killer hunt. She knows what happens when the
village isn't small enough. This is not "don't provoke the system." It is "I
know exactly what this man does."

The difficulty: Maia's personality is ferocious warmth --- she throws apples at
guards and hides. But with Kesal, something different takes over. The fierceness
is in the grip on Baret's arm, directed inward. This woman who throws sandals at
her sons cannot throw anything at this man. The reader should feel the
difference.

**Scene Detail**

**The feast itself.** Communal, not private. The entire village in a gathering
space inside the walls --- a square, an open area near the gate, wherever the
community assembles. Tables or benches. Food contributed from every household.
Fire. This is annual --- the village has done this for every departing cohort.
The routine is what gives it weight, the same way the blessing ceremony's
routine gives it weight. These people have feasted children before. Some of
those children didn't come back.

**The four to six candidates.** Named, known, specific. Every person at this
feast has watched these teenagers grow up. The baker taught them to make bread.
The smith fixed their tools. The old woman by the gate watched them run through
the village as children. The feast is not for candidates. It is for these
children --- Baret, Darek, and four others the reader will begin to know. The
specificity of a small village means the departure is personal three hundred
times over.

**Joy and dread.** The feast is genuinely warm. People are laughing, eating,
telling stories about the candidates as children. Embarrassing anecdotes, proud
moments. Darek would thrive here --- he's the center of warmth, telling stories,
making people laugh, probably helping prepare or serve the food (his food
obsession on full display). Baret is quieter, receiving the attention with his
characteristic reserve.

But the dread is present too. Some families at this feast have empty places ---
children who went to the Marking in previous years and didn't return, or
returned diminished. The older villagers carry the accumulated weight of every
departure feast they've attended. Nobody says this. The joy is real and the
dread is real and they coexist in the same room the way danger and routine
coexist on the frontier.

**Maia at the feast.** In her element --- and not. Her personality is ferocious
warmth, controlled chaos. At a village feast, she should be throwing food at her
boys, making scenes, being loudly, aggressively herself. The bright colors she
loves might be visible in whatever she's wearing or contributed. She throws
sandals at Baret when he says something she doesn't like. The crowd loves it.
She's performing. And it's almost perfect. But the reader has watched her pull
Baret away from Kesal hours ago. The performance has a hairline crack --- she's
sharper than usual, or too loud, or she holds Baret's shoulder a beat too long
when she passes behind him. The village sees a mother being emotional before her
sons leave. The reader sees a woman who watched a predator hunt today and is
holding herself together with her fingernails.

**Danik at the feast.** Quieter than Maia. Present, physical. The father from
the lion's claw story. He may exchange a look with Maia that says: I see you're
not okay. The parents carry Nalin's secret between them. At the feast,
surrounded by the village, they cannot speak it. They sit with three hundred
people and are completely alone in what they know.

**Nalin's absence at the feast.** Four candidates where there should have been
five --- or the absence felt in a different way. The sister would have been at
this feast. She would have been trial-age, or close to it. Maybe someone almost
says her name. Maybe a moment of silence that the village observes for lost
children touches the family differently. One year ago. The reader registers the
gap in the celebration.

**Darek at the feast.** This is his natural habitat. He's warm, funny, the
center of the social room. He might be helping serve food, making younger kids
laugh, teasing Baret in front of the village. His food obsession on display ---
he's brought the morels from the mountain and has opinions about how they should
be prepared. He critiques someone's cooking (not Maia's) and gets a ladle thrown
at him. The village sees the warm, sociable twin. The reader sees the brother
who will be publicly ranked below Baret tomorrow and doesn't know it yet.

**The girl from Scene 2.** She's at the feast. The whole village is here. She's
present in the room where Baret is being celebrated, hours after Kesal harassed
her and Baret did nothing. She doesn't confront him. She doesn't avoid him.
She's just there --- a person the village knows, carrying what happened to her,
participating in a celebration for other people's children. The reader holds
both truths: the village's warmth and the system's cruelty in the same room.

**The Escort News**

**The news arrives during the feast.** Word comes through Hangus, or through a
runner, or through village knowledge reaching the communal space: Kesal will
escort the Tanapun candidates to Pakran tomorrow morning. This is routine
protocol --- a retainer with jurisdiction escorts the village's young people to
the Marking. But the timing --- hours after the harassment --- and the public
setting --- the entire village watching --- create pressure.

**The village's reaction.** Unremarkable. Kesal is the expected escort. He has
jurisdiction. This is normal. Some parents might tighten slightly --- nobody
loves having their children under a retainer's authority for a day's travel ---
but it's routine. The village has sent children to Pakran under escort before.
The news doesn't disrupt the feast for anyone except the reader and Maia.

**Maia's reaction to the escort news.** This is the chapter's hardest moment.
She receives the news in front of three hundred people. She cannot react. She
cannot object. She cannot explain why this man specifically terrifies her.
Objecting would mean explaining what she knows about Kesal and Nalin, which she
cannot do without putting her sons at risk. She is trapped between knowledge and
silence, in public, at a feast celebrating her children.

What the village sees: a mother who tightens slightly at the news, the way every
mother tightens. Normal anxiety about the Marking. Maybe she busies herself with
food or conversation. Maybe she's louder for a moment, overcompensating.

What the reader sees: a mother who just watched her daughter's killer harass a
girl, and now learns he will have authority over her surviving children for a
day's travel through frontier territory. Every hour of that journey, Kesal will
be the armed authority responsible for Baret and Darek. She sends them anyway
because refusing the Marking is not an option. Everything she does for the rest
of the feast is performance.

On reread, after Chapter 13: this is the moment where the reader understands
what the series is built on. Maia has been "remaining" for a year --- carrying
knowledge that would destroy her family if spoken, performing warmth while the
system that killed her daughter operates around her. The feast is a celebration.
It is also the system working. And Maia is the only person in the room who knows
exactly how much the system costs.

**Baret at the feast after the news.** He doesn't know what Maia knows. He knows
what he saw: Kesal harassing a girl, hours ago. Now Kesal is the escort. The
dissonance sits in him without resolution. He's at a feast celebrating his
departure into a system that protects men like Kesal, and the man himself will
be his guardian tomorrow. Baret's silence pattern continues --- he doesn't raise
it, doesn't speak about it. Carries it.

**The lion's claw.** Visible or touched at some point during the feast. The
symbol of a father's protection. Danik is at this feast. The man who could fight
a lion. Nobody protected that girl today. The reader makes the connection.

**The feast winds down.** The blessing is at dawn. The village will sleep and
wake and perform the ceremony documented in the Chapter 3 outline. The chapter's
final image: the candidates --- four to six specific children --- in the
winding-down of a celebration held in their honor, carrying the warmth of three
hundred people who will touch their shoulders in the morning and then hand them
to Kesal.

**The Kesal Thread: Three-Chapter Pattern**

Kesal's predatory behavior planted across three chapters and connected in
Chapter 13.

**Chapter 1: The Horse (Planted Clue)**

Environmental detail. Kesal's horse in Tanapun before Nalin died. Invisible on
first read.

**Chapter 2: The Tanapun Girl (Witnessed, Survived)**

Kesal on patrol with armed men. Food theft. Harassment of known village girl
with soldiers behind him. Girl survives by accidental social intervention. Baret
witnesses both, does nothing. Maia pulls him away. Then at the feast: Kesal
named as escort. Maia controls her reaction in front of 300 people.

**Chapter 3: The Market Girl at Pakran (Barely Registered, Disappeared)**

Kesal escorts candidates to Pakran. Baret sees Kesal with a girl at a market
stall during arrival. Barely registers it. Later she's gone.

**Chapter 13: Baret Connects the Pattern**

After activation, Baret's enhanced cognition connects his own memories: the
horse, the Tanapun girl, the market girl, Nalin. Same man, same behavior. The AI
confirms the statistical analysis but cannot access the memories. The truth
comes from Baret.

**The revelation:** Nalin was not an isolated crime. She was one victim of a
serial predator who is still operating. Baret watched it happen and didn't see.

**Notes and Flags**

**Escalation Structure (Ch 1 → Ch 2 → Ch 3)**

Ch 1: Natural threats, Baret competent. Ch 2: Human cruelty backed by armed
force, Baret impotent, girl survives by accident. Feast celebrates children the
system may destroy. Escort news: predator becomes guardian. Ch 3: Same predator
escorts them through Ulreth territory, girl disappears at Pakran. Stakes
escalate, Baret's agency decreases.

**The Feast Replaces the Private Family Evening**

All family character moments happen within the communal feast: Maia's
personality, Darek cooking/serving, Danik's quiet presence, Nalin's absence, the
lion's claw callback. The feast forces Maia's performance to be public rather
than semi-private --- she controls herself in front of 300 people rather than 3
family members. More tension, more devastating on reread.

**Maia Has Three Devastating Moments**

\(1) Pulling Baret away from Kesal (Scene 2). (2) Performing warmth at the feast
while carrying what she knows (Scene 3). (3) Receiving the escort news in front
of the village (Scene 3). Each requires precision. The target: a rereader should
feel physically ill at all three.

**Kesal Must Not Be Monstrous**

Ordinary. Entitled. Food theft and harassment are the same gesture at different
scales. Banality = systemic.

**The Girl Needs Specificity**

Known village girl. Needs a name (vowel-initial per /h/-drop convention).
Present at the feast. Present at the blessing (Ch 3).

**The AI Mechanism (Corrected)**

Baret's enhanced cognition connects patterns in his own memories. AI cannot
access memories (hippocampal degradation). AI confirms analysis. Truth comes
from Baret.

**Pacing**

Three scenes, same day as Chapter 1. Estimate 3,500--5,000 words. Scene 1 is
quick. Scene 2 is the weight-bearing center (Kesal). Scene 3 is the emotional
center (feast + escort news). The feast scene may run long --- if it pushes past
2,000 words, tighten the communal texture and let the escort news land faster.
